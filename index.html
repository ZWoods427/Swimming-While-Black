Swimming While Black: A look at the sport's racist history and promising future.

Learning how to swim has many benefits for people of all ages. The ability to safely enjoy water activities in the summer, physical fitness and stress relief are just a few examples of why many people take up the hobby. According to Stop Drowning Now roughly 3,400 people drown each year with drowning being the leading cause of deaths for children between the ages of one and four. Of those fatalities, sixty-four percent of Black children have little to no swimming skills compared to forty-five percent of Hispanic/Latino and forty percent of caucasian children. The solution to this issue is to teach children and adults how to swim.

There is a long standing stereotype that Black Americans do not know or are unable to learn how to swim. This assumption has existed for many decades with most people of all races believing it to be true. And while the statistics validate this assumption, the reasons for the high percentage are deeply rooted in this country’s history of segregation and racism. We will take a closer look at the history of America’s swimming pools and the progress made towards bridging the swimming gap.
